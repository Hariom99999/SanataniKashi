GOD

The first thing to understand is that the concept of god in Sanatana Darma is not the same as in Abrahamic religions. Mistakes are made and misunderstandings engender when we try to translate the concepts with a view of making the followers of Abrahamic religions understand it in accordance with their templates. What should be done is to first introduce them to our thought patterns instead of trying to translate it in their terms. Even Brahma in no way equates to God. Practitioners of Sanatana Dharma have different understandings about the entities that are of different natures in Brahmanda (it is again not the ‘Universe). The most widely accepted descriptions of these entities can be understood in the following way. Ishwara or Parameshwara or Parambrahma is aptly defined in YogaSutra of Patanjali as ‘Klesha-karma-vipakaashayairaparamrishtah purush vishesh Ishwarah’. This means that Ishwara is an entity qualified as something that is entirely untouched or uninfluenced in any way by “klesha”- outcome of instable and inharmonious emotions, Karma – actions or resolutions for action, Vipaka – results (that usually come out from or mature as results of actions), ashaya – desires, hopes or resolutions of beginnings. Such an entity is all pervading hence omnipotent but entirely non-intervening. Such an entity cannot be worshipped and only ‘upasana’ (staying silent in its side or near it in a meditative state basking in its glory) can be done. This action simply results in removal all complexes, obstructions and hindrances in the flow of life (this might not be in congruence with our wishes and desires, which in fact are nothing but accumulation through interactions and influences. However, it will certainly be shreyaskar and for our ultimate good), this is considered to be the highest of all achievements.
Now about the entities termed as Devatas and Devis, according to our traditions they are entities that are basically providers, givers or donors. Whoever or whatever provides for in whichever way for our life and our sustenance is a Devata, the level of divinity corresponds to the capacity of provisions that can be made by these entities. According to this definitions all our benefactors, be they humans, animals or even inanimate objects can qualify as being Devatas. This is the concept when we say that there are innumerable Devatas in so called Hindu pantheon. When defining and categorising the devatas many reference points have been used by different sages (Just as the botanists and zoologists do with references to genus, family and species etc.). Some categorize them in thousands while using a different reference frame, some in 33 categories (33 kotis mean 33 categories but which have been understood by many as 330 million as koti is a word used for categories and a number equivalent to 10 million.), some in seven, six, three and half, three, two and one and even as nothingness. Out of these 33 category standard and reference has found wide acceptance as it is the easiest to understand. Hence we shall be expounding on them.
According to some oral traditions and the most logical ones, the number 33 is divided as 8 categories of Vasus, 11 categories of Rudras, 12 categories of Adityas, 1 Prajapati and 1 Brahma. The traditions explain that Vasus are primarily (not absolutely) the authorities over space (though they have authorities over other aspects also), the Adityas are the authorities that primarily oversee the time (though they have authority over other aspects as well), The Rudras are primarily authorities over Crying that is, the result of overflow of emotions. Prajapati is the one who manifests and expands so that the earlier three can play out their roles and exercise their authorities. And lastly Indra, the authority over our Indriyas or senses to perceive, feel and enjoy the forementioned interplays.
Similarly under some traditions another entity that is termed as Bhagwan can be understood with the help of a shloka from Vishnu purana.
aiśvaryasya samagrasya
vīryasya yaśasaḥ śriyaḥ
jñāna-vairāgyayoś caiva
ṣaṇṇāṁ bhaga itīṅganā
(Viṣṇu Purāṇa 6.5.47)
According to this shloka ‘bhaga’ is the name given to six opulent attributes, any entity with these six attributes in their entirety is the Bhagawan. Only the Ultimate can hold these attributes. However, according to some traditions the extent that any entity holds these attributes has corresponding Bhagawatta within it.
When The Ultimate is envisaged in this way It becomes an entity that can be worshipped and with whom a relationship can be struck. This way Bhagwan becomes the omnipotent Devata as well and we can receive its grace.
The above concepts become abstruse and muddled when we try to understand them within the Abrahamic template and their concept of God. According to us the Ultimate is Ultimate and it can be anything a father, a son, a friend, a lover and even an enemy (for Asuras like Ravana and others of his ilk). Once we understand this paradox we can understand their stories and can figure out that even if they were great scholars, had extraordinary capabilities and were devotees still the Ultimate in its Leelas was an adversary.
The problem arises when we are unaware of this and with deeply engrained Decartesian concepts and exclusivist mindsets we have been exposed to. For a dharmik person there is no problem if a group accepts the Ultimate as a Father, or if some other group says that he is the only one and is omnipotent.
